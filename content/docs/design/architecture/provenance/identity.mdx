# The Identity Model

The [Security](/docs/design/architecture/security) page insists on a sharp line between **you**
and **the machine**, even when the machine acts on your behalf. That line is not a slogan — it is
a data structure. Every task Arkitekt records carries **four distinct identities**, and the
platform deliberately refuses to collapse them into a single "user did this".

## Four identities, one change

When a change is recorded under a task, the task answers four different questions:

| Question | Identity | Token claim |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| On whose **authority** does this ultimately run? | **Root human causer** | `rcb` |
| Who triggered **this particular hop**? | **Immediate caller** | `sub` |
| Which **machine** actually did the work? | **Executing agent** | `act` (`act.sub` + `act.cid`) |
| Who **vouched** for all of it? | **Issuer** | `iss` |

### Root human causer — the authority

The root human causer is the person at the very root of the causal chain — the human whose
decision set everything in motion. No matter how many automated hops sit between that decision and
the final change, the authority is always attributed back to this human. This is the answer to
*"on whose authority?"*, and it is the identity that never gets to hide behind an agent.

When that human is a known local user, the task resolves to their account; when they are external
or unknown, the task still records the raw identity rather than silently dropping it (see
[boundaries](/docs/design/architecture/provenance/effective-changes#the-edges)).

### Immediate caller — this hop's trigger

The immediate caller is whoever triggered *this specific task*. In a simple, one-step action the
immediate caller and the root human causer are the same person. But once tasks chain — an agent
calling another agent — the immediate caller of a downstream task is the **upstream agent**, not
the original human. Keeping this separate from the root causer is what lets you read a chain hop
by hop without losing track of where ultimate authority lies.

### Executing agent — the machine

The executing agent is the machine that actually performed the work: its user identity (`act.sub`)
and its OAuth client (`act.cid`). This is the *"by what"* of the audit trail. When an AI agent
runs a node and writes a result, the executing agent is recorded as the doer — explicitly, and
never merged into the human who authorized it.

### Issuer — the attestor

The issuer is the authority that minted and signed the provenance token. It is the part of the
trail that says *"this attribution is vouched for"*, and it underpins the
[integrity guarantees](/docs/design/architecture/provenance/integrity): an attribution is only as
trustworthy as the issuer that signed it.

## Why keep them apart?

Because conflating them destroys exactly the trust provenance exists to protect. "A human
authorized this" and "a machine carried it out" are *both true at once*, and a record that keeps
only one of those facts is a record that lies by omission. By storing all four identities
side by side, Arkitekt can always answer, later and with confidence:

> *Agent **X**, triggered by **Y**, did this on the ultimate authority of human **Z**, attested
> by issuer **W**.*

That sentence is the whole point — and it is only sayable because the four identities were never
allowed to merge.
