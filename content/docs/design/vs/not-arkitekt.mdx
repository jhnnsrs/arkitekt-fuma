# What Arkitekt is not

Arkitekt is often mistaken for tools it deliberately is *not*. People reach for
the comparison they already know — "so it's like Dask?", "is this just
Nextflow?", "are you doing ROS?" — and are surprised when Arkitekt behaves
differently.

So let's draw those boundaries clearly. But first, the important part:

<Callout type="info" title="We love these projects">

Dask, Nextflow and ROS are some of the best engineering the scientific-computing
community has ever produced. We use them, we learn from them, and Arkitekt is
better *because* they exist. Nothing on this page is a knock on them — it is a
map of where each of them shines and where Arkitekt picks up.

</Callout>

In the spirit of the rest of the [Vs series](/docs/design/vs), this is not about
Arkitekt being *better* than these tools. It is the opposite: each is superb at
its job, and the right answer is almost always to run Arkitekt **with** them, not
instead of them.

So what *is* Arkitekt? It is a **reactive platform** that sits between your data,
your tools and your team — a stateful middleman that turns scattered scripts and
instruments into live, composable apps. That framing is the key to every
comparison below.

## Not a parallelization library (Dask)

We adore [Dask](https://www.dask.org/). It is one of the cleanest answers in the
Python ecosystem to a genuinely hard problem: scaling array, dataframe and
task-graph computation across many cores and machines. You hand it a big
computation, it splits the work, schedules it, and hands you the result.

Arkitekt is **not a pure parallelization library** — and never wants to be. We
are a *reactive platform built on a concurrency model*. The unit of work in
Arkitekt is not a transient task in a graph you submit and forget; it is a
long-lived, stateful **app** that stays alive, holds context (a loaded model, an
open instrument connection, a running viewer) and brokers data and compute
between other apps and your team.

That is a different problem from "split this computation across a cluster" — and
when you *do* need raw parallel compute, the last thing we want is to reinvent
Dask badly. We just call it.

<Callout type="info" title="We integrate, we don't compete">

Need on-demand parallel compute from inside an app? **Kluster** is Arkitekt's
gateway for spinning up Dask clusters (on SLURM, PBS, Kubernetes, …) on demand.
See [Kluster](/docs/design/services/next/kluster).

</Callout>

## Not just stateless execution (Nextflow)

[Nextflow](https://www.nextflow.io/) is, frankly, a triumph. For reproducible,
**stateless** batch pipelines on a cluster it is hard to beat — describe your
processes in a clean DSL, point it at your infrastructure, and it reliably grinds
through enormous batch jobs where every step starts fresh and exits when done.
We borrowed more than a few good ideas from it.

Arkitekt is **not "just" a stateless executor**. Smart microscopy is rarely
stateless: a deep-learning model is loaded once and reused across thousands of
frames, an instrument stays connected, and — crucially — *the result of one step
steers the next acquisition*. That live, closed feedback loop does not map onto a
fire-and-forget batch process. Arkitekt apps stay alive, keep their state, and
talk through messages, so the model loads once and the loop stays tight.

This statefulness is a double-edged sword (a leaking app keeps leaking; a batch
process just exits), so it does not make Arkitekt "better" — it makes it suited
to a different shape of problem. For big, embarrassingly parallel batch science,
reach for Nextflow with our blessing.

<Callout type="info" title="We integrate, we don't compete">

Already living in Nextflow, or want the stateless batch model? Read the deeper
[Arkitekt vs Nextflow](/docs/design/vs/nextflow) comparison, and the
[Nextflow integration strategies](/docs/roadmap/thoughts/nextflow) for calling
Arkitekt apps from Nextflow workflows and back.

</Callout>

## Not robotic swarm orchestration (ROS)

[ROS](https://www.ros.org/) is the backbone of modern robotics for a reason. It
owns real-time control and swarm orchestration — coordinating motors, sensors and
actuators down at the hardware, with the timing guarantees that demands. It is
extraordinary, hard-won engineering, and we have nothing but respect for it.

Arkitekt is **not robotics middleware**, and we would never pretend otherwise. We
do not orchestrate a robot's joints, and we do not coordinate a swarm in real
time — that layer is firmly out of scope, and ROS is exactly the right tool for
it.

But the moment your robot (or microscope, or stage) exposes a high-level
**action** — "pick the sample", "move to position", "run the scan" — Arkitekt
becomes your friend. Wrap that action as an app and it turns into a node you can
compose into reactive workflows alongside your analysis and your data. ROS drives
the metal; Arkitekt choreographs the science on top.

<Callout type="info" title="We integrate, we don't compete">

Once your hardware speaks in actions, expose it as an Arkitekt
[app](/docs/apps) and orchestrate it together with the rest of your lab.

</Callout>
